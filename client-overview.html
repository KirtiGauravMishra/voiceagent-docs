In plain terms

Ben fills out a form on your website and becomes a Lead. Within minutes, Aria — your sales agent — calls him. If he doesn't pick up, a Workflow automatically tries again in two hours, no one has to remember to. If Ben's ready to move forward, Aria books the appointment straight into your calendar during the call — no back-and-forth emails, no double-booking. If instead it's urgent, or Ben simply asks for a real person, Aria hands the call off to your team immediately, mid-call, so nothing important is ever left to a bot alone. The moment the call ends, the transcript, recording, and outcome are saved, Ben's spot in your pipeline updates itself, and it all rolls up onto your Dashboard — so by the end of the day you know exactly how many leads were reached, how many appointments were booked, and how many needed a human, without pulling a report together by hand.

How long this takes to build

Two honest paths, depending on how the voice pipeline itself gets built. Treat these as planning estimates, not a committed schedule, until scope is confirmed in a kickoff call.

Working days = Monday–Friday only. Weekends aren't counted, so the working-week total won't match a plain calendar-week count.

Replit's agent compresses every section, since most of it is scaffolding — CRUD screens, API routes, data models — that it can generate directly from the detailed specs we've already written. It compresses the real-time voice pipeline less: that section still needs live testing, latency tuning, and debugging timing issues across the AI providers involved, which is review work, not typing work. With ElevenLabs' ready-made agent, the speech recognition, voice synthesis, and turn-taking are already built and hosted for us — even the agent has less to wire up there. Building it ourselves means directing the agent through that entire real-time pipeline, still this project's single most complex piece of work even with AI-assisted development.

The trade-off isn't only speed. The ready-made path launches faster, but every call then runs on ElevenLabs' pricing and infrastructure indefinitely — see the cost comparison on the next page. Building it ourselves takes a bit longer up front but means we control latency, can swap in cheaper providers later without a rebuild, and aren't dependent on one vendor's roadmap, rate changes, or uptime.

These are estimates, not quotes. Rates were checked against each provider's official pricing pages in July 2026 and drift over time — confirm current rates directly with each vendor before finalizing a budget. Cartesia's Voice Agents rate ($0.06/min call duration + $0.014/min telephony) and its standalone Sonic-3.5 TTS rate ($0.0294/min of generated audio, Startup plan) are both taken directly from their published plan comparison table. Converting Sonic's per-generated-audio-minute rate into a per-call-minute figure assumes the agent is actively speaking roughly half of each call — a reasonable estimate, but softer than ElevenLabs' figure, which is priced directly per character with no conversion needed. Figures also assume about 4 LLM exchanges per minute with rolling conversation history and roughly 1MB of recorded audio per call-minute for storage sizing. Recording cost isn't listed on Cartesia's own pricing page for either option, so it's estimated at Twilio's published rate. LLM usage during Cartesia Voice Agents calls is currently free for UI-built agents as a limited-time promotion — modeled here at its steady-state cost instead, since a promotional rate isn't a safe basis for a long-term budget. All figures exclude the one-time engineering cost of building Option B (see the previous page).

Why the numbers moved around: Cartesia's ready-made Voice Agents product isn't the cheapest way to use Cartesia — telephony is billed on top of the $0.06/min call rate, not included in it, which pushes it above ElevenLabs' bundled telephony+recording rate on a per-item basis (though still well under ElevenLabs' overall total). The cheapest path in this comparison is building the pipeline ourselves using Cartesia purely as the voice engine: their standalone Sonic TTS, priced per minute of audio actually generated rather than per minute of call time, works out cheaper than paying for their fully-bundled agent product or for ElevenLabs' standalone TTS API.
